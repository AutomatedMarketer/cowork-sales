# Sequence Variant C: Character Story Email

Use when you want to build deep brand trust through vulnerability and specificity. A single character story email can outperform an entire nurture sequence if the story is true, specific, and lands correctly on the offer.

## The format

```
[Opening: a vulnerable moment — specific, named, real]

[Middle: tie that moment to the audience's experience — their version of the same feeling]

[Turn: the lesson — what changed and why]

[Close: the offer — arrived at naturally through the story, not bolted on]
```

## Rules

1. **Must be true.** No composite stories, no hypotheticals. If it didn't happen to you, don't use it.
2. **Must be specific.** Date, place, person present, exact quote if possible. Vague vulnerability is not vulnerability.
3. **Must connect to the offer.** The story must logically arrive at the offer. If you have to force the transition, the story is wrong.
4. **One story per email.** Never stack two character moments. The second one dilutes the first.
5. **The offer appears once.** Never repeat it or amp up urgency within the same email.

## Template

```
Subject: <The moment — stated plainly, no drama>

[Paragraph 1 — The scene: where were you, what were you doing, what happened]
Aim for 40-60 words. Plant the reader in the specific moment.

[Paragraph 2 — The feeling: what you thought, feared, or were ashamed of]
This is where the reader recognizes themselves. Be precise about the internal state.

[Paragraph 3 — The connection: their version of this moment]
Name the audience's experience directly. "If you've ever..." or "This is the exact moment
most [audience] stop moving."

[Paragraph 4 — The turn: what changed and the specific reason why]
One concrete action, one concrete result. Name the number. Name the timeframe.

[Paragraph 5 — The bridge to the offer]
One sentence that arrives at the offer naturally. Not "and that's why I built X" —
instead: "The method I used to get through that is exactly what's inside X."

[Offer line: one sentence + one link]

[Optional P.S. — a second proof point or a hard deadline if applicable]
```

## Example character story email

```
Subject: I almost quit on a Sunday in October

It was October 12th, 2022. I'd been building for 11 months.

I was sitting at my kitchen table at 6am with a spreadsheet open
showing $2,300 in revenue for the entire year. My son was still
asleep. My wife didn't know how bad the numbers were.

I wrote a draft email to my list that said: "I'm pausing this."

I didn't send it. But I sat with it open for 47 minutes.

If you've ever been the only person in the room who knows how close
to done you actually are — that specific Sunday feeling — this is
for you.

What I did instead of sending that email changed everything. I made
one list. Not a to-do list. A "what is already working" list. I found
three things. I wrote one offer around the smallest one.

It made $4,100 in 9 days.

The framework I built from that Sunday is now inside the Revenue
Reset workshop. It's 90 minutes. It's the only thing I'd go back
and give myself that morning.

Details here: [link]

P.S. That worksheet I made on October 12th is included. Exactly
as I wrote it that morning.
```

## When to use character story format

| Situation | Fits? |
|-----------|-------|
| Cold list you haven't mailed in 60+ days | Yes — story re-earns trust before the offer |
| Warm list mid-launch (day 2 or 3) | Yes — adds humanity between proof emails |
| Hard pitch after a webinar | Yes — softens the transition from event to email |
| Weekly newsletter anchor | Yes — one story per week builds consistent brand identity |
| Day 1 of any sequence | Risky — save story for day 2 after the hook lands |

## Common mistakes

- **Vague vulnerability**: "I had a hard year" → not a story. Name the year, the month, the specific thing.
- **Hero arc too fast**: Moving from crisis to triumph in 3 sentences feels fake. Let the struggle breathe.
- **Offer forced on**: If you have to write "and that's why I created [offer]..." — the story and offer aren't connected. Rebuild the bridge.
- **Too long**: Character story emails over 350 words typically lose the thread. Cut to the bone.
