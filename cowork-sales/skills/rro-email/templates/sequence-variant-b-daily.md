# Sequence Variant B: Daily Entertainment Email

Use when you want to build a list relationship over time. The format is low-sell, high-story — the offer lives in the P.S. and feels like an afterthought even though it's the conversion mechanism.

## The format

```
[Opening: relatable observation — 30-50 words, a real moment from your life]

[Body: one value tip — specific, actionable, 60-100 words]

[P.S.: the offer — 1-2 sentences, soft, almost incidental]
```

## Rules

- Write daily. Missing a day breaks the trust compound. Schedule in advance if needed.
- The opening must be specific — a real moment, not a category ("I was on a call today" is weak; "I was on a Zoom with my accountant at 9am when she said something I've been thinking about since" is strong).
- The body tip must be immediately actionable. If the reader can't use it today, it's too abstract.
- The P.S. is where the offer lives. It should feel like you remembered at the last second. It never has urgency language. It's just there.
- Subject lines: short, curious, personal. Never promotional. Think "something you'd text a friend."

## Subject line patterns for daily format

- **Observation**: "my accountant said something weird this morning"
- **Result**: "3 clients, same week, same mistake"
- **Confessional**: "I was wrong about this for 4 years"
- **Question**: "does this happen to you too?"

Avoid: anything with ALL CAPS, exclamation points, brackets with promises like [FREE], or phrases like "Don't miss".

## Example email 1: Client observation opening

```
Subject: client said something that stopped me cold

I was reviewing a proposal draft with a client yesterday when she paused
and said: "This is everything I want, but I'm afraid to send it."

Her offer was excellent. Her proof was solid. She was afraid of the reply.

The fix we used: cut the proposal from 4 pages to 1 page, move the price
to line 3, and remove all the "I hope this finds you well" softening.
She sent it in 6 minutes. Client said yes the same afternoon.

If your proposals feel like essays, that's usually the signal to cut harder,
not add more.

## Example email 2: Personal mistake opening

```
Subject: I messed up a client's launch week

Three weeks ago I gave a client advice that cost her a day of momentum.

She asked whether to send her launch email on Tuesday or Thursday.
I said Thursday — better open rates historically.

She launched Thursday. Her audience is mostly US East Coast small business
owners who are slammed on Thursdays. Opens were 40% below her normal rate.

The lesson isn't Tuesday vs Thursday. It's: I should have asked "when does
YOUR audience have slack time?" before I answered with a generic benchmark.

Default to asking over answering. Gets better results and builds more trust.

## Cadence guidance

| Situation | Recommendation |
|-----------|----------------|
| New list (0-90 days) | Daily — build the habit of opening |
| Established list (90+ days) | 5x/week minimum — drops below 3x loses trust |
| Re-engagement (list gone cold) | Daily for 2 weeks, then evaluate open rates |
| Launch week | Daily — email IS your launch |

## What to do when you run out of ideas

Daily email idea sources (never run dry if you use these):
1. One real thing that happened today — a call, a mistake, a client win
2. One belief you held last year that you now think is wrong
3. One question a client asked this week that surprised you
4. One thing you noticed working differently than you expected
5. One result — yours or a client's — with the specific cause named
