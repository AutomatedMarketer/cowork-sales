# Awareness Level 2: Problem-Aware

## Who they are
They know something is wrong — they feel the friction, the frustration, the cost — but they haven't connected it to a fixable problem yet, and they haven't started searching for solutions.

## What they know
The symptoms. Not the diagnosis. They know they're losing time, money, or momentum, but they haven't framed it as a solvable category.

## Lead with
Name the problem precisely and without softening. They'll feel seen the moment you describe their specific daily friction in words they recognize. Don't pitch the solution yet — just show them you understand the problem better than they do.

## Sample opener
```
If your day starts with 47 unread emails and you don't know which
to answer first — that's not a time management problem.
That's a prioritization system problem. Different fix entirely.
```

Or:

```
Most consultants losing clients after month 3 think the problem is
their delivery. It's usually their onboarding. The churn happens in
week 1, not month 3.
```

## CTA strength
**Soft to medium.** A reply asking about their experience works well. A link to a free diagnostic or framework is appropriate. An offer for a paid product is premature unless it's very low-ticket and low-commitment.

## Subject line approach
- Problem framing: "why your proposals keep getting ghosted"
- Diagnosis: "it's not a time problem — it's a sequencing problem"
- Challenge: "the thing most [audience] are blaming the wrong cause for"

## When to use this calibration
- Warm leads who've engaged with content (opened emails, clicked links) but haven't bought
- Webinar registrants who haven't attended yet
- Followers who've been watching your content but not inquiring
- Early subscribers (30-60 days on list) still in the trust-building phase
